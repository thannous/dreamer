Dream dictionary app for Android

Updated July 9, 2026

Noctalia combines a public dictionary of 150 editorial dream-symbol entries with an Android journal. Look up a symbol for possible meanings, then bring it back to the full dream, your emotions and the details you captured on waking.

Use a dream dictionary as a starting point

A symbol page can help you find language for an image such as water, a dog, a door or a bridge. It cannot know what that image means to you. Read the common possibilities, note which variation appeared and check the emotion in the dream before choosing an interpretation that fits.

1. Look up

Browse 150 public symbol entries by category or A-Z. Each entry presents variations, questions and possible associations.

2. Add context

Record what the symbol did, how it looked and how you felt. A calm sea and a flood should not be read as the same scene.

3. Compare

Save the complete dream in the app and revisit repeated symbols across entries instead of making a decision from one lookup.

Editorial and privacy limits

The dictionary is editorial content, not a dataset of private user dreams and not clinical guidance. Noctalia does not present a clinician as reviewer of this page. When you use the Android journal, the privacy policy explains data hosting, AI providers and deletion controls.

Dream dictionary FAQ

Is the Noctalia dream dictionary free to browse?

Yes. The public web dictionary lets you browse 150 editorial dream-symbol entries by category or A-Z without installing the app.

Does one symbol always have the same meaning?

No. The same symbol can relate to different memories, emotions and situations. Use the dictionary as a set of possibilities and compare them with the complete dream.

What does the Android app add to the dictionary?

The app adds voice or text capture, a private journal, AI reflection, mood and symbol context, generated images and follow-up questions around your saved dream.

Can a dream dictionary diagnose or predict something?

No. Dream-dictionary entries are informational prompts, not medical advice, psychological diagnosis or predictions.
